I then spent four years as a PhD candidate at the *Ecole Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne* ([EPFL](https://www.epfl.ch/en/)) in Lausanne, Switzerland. My main research focuses on understanding and characterizing plasma turbulence in magnetic confinement fusion devices. This is a crucial research topic, as turbulence remains one of the major uncertainties towards the exploitation of fusion energy on Earth. 

## Fusion Energy

Fusion stands out as one of the most promising options for generating clean and sustainable energy to meet humanity's growing energy needs. 
When two light nuclei merge, part of their mass transforms into energy, as described by Einstein's famous equation $$E=m c^2$$. One of the most promising fusion reactions involves Deuterium and Tritium, two heavier isotopes of Hydrogen. This process offers several significant benefits:

-	**Clean energy**:  Each fusion reaction produces a Helium nucleus and a neutron. Helium has various applications in industry and medicine (or can be used for inflating children's balloons). Neutrons are slowed down in specially designed mantles, converting their energy into electricity through standard techniques. *Fusion power plants produce no CO$$_2$$ emissions.*
-	**Abundant fuel**: Just one gram of Deuterium and Tritium could generate 90,000 kilowatt-hours of energy, equivalent to burning 11 tonnes of coal. Deuterium can be extracted from seawater, while Tritium can be obtained from Lithium within fusion power plants, ensuring an abundant fuel supply on Earth.
-	**Reliable power**: Fusion power plants can operate continuously, regardless of time of day or weather conditions, providing a constant supply of electricity and warm water for remote heating. 
-	**Safe operation**: Fusion does not involve chain reactions. If any disturbance occurs, the plasma cools down, and the fusion reaction stops immediately. Additionally, the amount of fuel present in fusion power plants at any given time will be very small, further enhancing safety.

Along with other renewable energy sources, fusion will provide a pivotal contribution to the decarbonization. 

Fusion reactions require extreme temperatures to occur. At these high temperatures, matter exists in the state of plasma. There are two main approaches to confine this high-temperature fusion plasma: inertial confinement and magnetic confinement. In the latter, strong magnetic fields are used to confine the charged particles in a toroidal machine. A steep temperature gradient exists in this setup, with the plasma core reaching hundreds of millions of degrees where fusion reactions occur, while the temperature decreases to a few hundred degrees at the machine vessel where particles and heat are exhausted. This extreme temperature difference generates a set of instabilities, leading to turbulent transport within the plasma. 

One of the most significant international research collaborations in fusion is the ITER project. This collaboration brings together China, the European Union, India, Japan, Korea, Russia, and the United States. ITER is a large-scale experimental facility currently under construction in southern France with the main goal to demonstrate the technological feasibility of fusion as sustainable energy source. Find out more about ITER and fusion energy in the [ITER website](https://www.iter.org/)!

## Plasma Turbulence

Turbulence is one of Nature's most fascinating phenomena, ubiquitous in our daily lives. From stirring your teacup to riding your bike to work, you constantly interact with turbulence. This complex phenomenon arises from nonlinear processes and is often accompanied by chaos, making its dynamics exceptionally challenging to understand. Consider the stock market as an example of turbulence in a different context. A single news article can trigger unpredictable effects, causing some investors to gain fortunes while others suffer significant losses. As a matter of fact, the risks and opportunities in the stock market are largely associated with chaos and unpredictability.

Turbulence in a fusion plasma transports particles and heat from the plasma core to the boundary region, effectively determining how good the plasma confinement is. Large turbulent transport results in poor plasma confinement and, therefore, low fusion performance. Can we predict turbulent transport in magnetic confinement fusion machines? The answer is yes, but it is not a simple task. It requires performing highly complex turbulence simulations that demand the computational power of the world's largest high-performance computing facilities.
