I have been working on plasma turbulence since many years. This is a fascinating yet very challenging research topic, with applications than span from astrophysical to fusion plasmas. The primary application of my research activities is on magnetic confinement plasma devices, which are toroidal machines in which a hot plasma is confined by means of strong magnetic fields. Tokamak, stellarator and reversed field pinch are the three main magnetic configurations, which differ on how the magnetic field is generated. In tokamaks, the magnetic field in the toroidal direction, i.e., parallel to the torus axis, is generated through external coils, while the poloidal magnetic field is mostly produced by a toroidal current flowing in the plasma. In stellarators, the confining magnetic field is almost entirely produced through external three-dimensional coils. On opposite, the magnetic field in reversed field pinch configurations is mostly self-generated by the plasma. 

From a topological perspective, the plasma domain in magnetic confinement plasma devices is divided in two regions: a central region characterized by nested magnetic flux surfaces where temperature reaches values of the order of 100 million degrees, and the boundary region where the magnetic field lines intercept the vessel wall. Turbulence in the two regions is typically investigated with a different model, which target the different plasma parameters and magnetic geometries.

## Core turbulence

The fusion performance in magnetic confinement plasma devices is proportional to the energy confinement time, namely the time the energy takes to leave the confined plasma. Higher the energy confinement time, larger is the fusion efficiency. An accurate evaluation of the energy confinement time is essential to design and operate any magnetic confinement fusion device. Unfortunately, the energy confinement time depends on turbulent transport, whose evaluation requires performing expensive three-dimensional gyrokinetic simulations. Gyrokinetic models provide the most accurate way to describe turbulence in fusion plasma. On the other hand, gyrokinetic simulations are computationally expensive. They require running a gyrokinetic code for several day on thousands on cores in high-performance computing facilities. On the other hand, the continuous progress in the development of large-scale high-performance computing facilities has dramatically improved the capability to routinely carry out gyrokinetic simulations, enabling high-fidelity predictions of the energy confinement time in future fusion power plants. Gyrokinetic simulations are not only used to evaluate the energy confinement time in magnetic confinement fusion devices, but they also provide the most advanced tools to characterize plasma turbulence in magnetized plasmas, helping us to advance our knowledge on this fascinating, yet very complex, research topic.
