I have been working on plasma turbulence since many years. This is a fascinating yet very challenging research topic, with applications than span from astrophysical to fusion plasmas. The primary application of my research activities is on magnetic confinement plasma devices, which are toroidal machines in which a hot plasma is confined by means of strong magnetic fields. Tokamak, stellarator and reversed field pinch are the three main magnetic configurations, which differ on how the magnetic field is generated. In tokamaks, the magnetic field in the toroidal direction, i.e., parallel to the torus axis, is generated through external coils, while the poloidal magnetic field is mostly produced by a toroidal current flowing in the plasma. In stellarators, the confining magnetic field is almost entirely produced through external three-dimensional coils. On opposite, the magnetic field in reversed field pinch configurations is mostly self-generated by the plasma. 

From a topological perspective, the plasma domain in magnetic confinement plasma devices is divided in two regions: a central region characterized by nested magnetic flux surfaces where temperature reaches values of the order of 100 million degrees, and the boundary region where the magnetic field lines intercept the vessel wall. Turbulence in the two regions is typically investigated with a different model, which target the different plasma parameters and magnetic geometries.

## Core turbulence

The fusion performance in magnetic confinement plasma devices is proportional to the energy confinement time, namely the time the energy takes to leave the confined plasma. Higher the energy confinement time, larger is the fusion efficiency. An accurate evaluation of the energy confinement time is essential to design and operate any magnetic confinement fusion device. Unfortunately, the energy confinement time depends on turbulent transport, whose evaluation requires performing expensive three-dimensional gyrokinetic simulations. Gyrokinetic models provide the most accurate way to describe turbulence in fusion plasma, but gyrokinetic simulations are computationally expensive. However, the continuous progress in the development of large-scale high-performance computing facilities has dramatically improved the capability to routinely carry out gyrokinetic simulations, enabling high-fidelity predictions of the energy confinement time in future fusion power plants. Gyrokinetic simulations are not only used to evaluate the energy confinement time in magnetic confinement fusion devices, but they also provide the most advanced tools to characterize plasma turbulence in magnetized plasmas, helping us to advance our knowledge on this fascinating, yet very complex, research topic.          

## Boundary turbulence

The external plasma region is the region where the plasma interacts with the vessel wall. This region is critical for many aspects, since it regulates the heat exhaust and controls the overall fusion performance. This region is characterized by very steep density and temperature gradients, which enable a variety of plasma instabilities that develop nonlinearly. The strongly-nonlinear dynamics is accompanied by a complex geometry of the magnetic field lines, which are opportunely shaped to channel heat and particles coming from the core plasma into specific wall regions, which are designed to withstand heat fluxes that are larger than those affecting a shuttle during the re-entry into the atmosphere. The design of this region is delicate and require an accurate prediction of turbulent transport. This is usually achieved by performing expensive three-dimensional global turbulence simulations, which allow for a detailed characterization of plasma turbulence in complex magnetic geometries. ## Reduced models in the boundary region

Three-dimensional simulations provide plenty of details but are terribly expensive! They are commonly run on thousands of CPUs for months. The design of a magnetic confinement fusion device requires probing a large parameter space to find an optimal operating point, which is a compromise among many constraints. Performing turbulence simulations for every possible operating point is practically impossible. For this reason, reduced physical models are used as a proxy of more sophisticate (high-fidelity) three-dimensional fluid or gyrokinetic models. Developing accurate reduced transport models is essential. In fact, large uncertainties on the prediction of turbulent transport in the external plasma region translate into high costs of magnetic confinement fusion devices. The European Commission, through EURATOM-Horizon Europe, has funded a 600 k€ project on developing reduced transport models in the external plasma region. I am pleased to be the Principal Investigator of this project, which involves three European research institutes, *Consorzio RFX* (Italy), the *Dutch Institute for Fundamental Energy Research* (the Netherlands) and the *Swiss Plasma Center – EPFL* (Switzerland).
